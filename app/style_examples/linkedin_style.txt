Your RAG pipeline is carrying 30GB it doesn’t need to.

10 million documents. Standard embeddings. You do the math and land around 30GB of RAM always warm, always costing you.

Most teams ignore and provision bigger servers. That’s the expensive shrug.


TurboQuant dropped in March 2026. Google Research. ICLR accepted.

Not a startup. Not a weekend Medium post. A peer-reviewed algorithm that made memory chip stocks fall the day it published, SK Hynix down 6%, Samsung down 5%.

The core idea: compress your vectors before storing them. 
Not the documents the raw float values. 
Down to 2.5–3.5 bits per channel.
At 3.5 bits, quality is essentially neutral. 

The 30GB → 4GB number on that board? Plausible but bit-width dependent. 

Your actual savings depend on where you draw that line.


What makes it different from FAISS/PQ.

Traditional Product Quantization requires offline training cluster your data, build codebooks, reindex whenever things change.

TurboQuant’s codebooks are data-oblivious. No training. No calibration. Vectors compress as they arrive. New document added? Compressed immediately. No batch jobs.

For a growing RAG corpus, that’s operationally significant.


Where I’d pump the brakes.

TurboQuant’s strongest benchmarks are around KV cache compression for LLM inference that’s where the paper is tightest.

For RAG vector stores specifically, one developer implementing Stage 2 on standard sentence embeddings found recall actually got worse. 
The algorithm behaves differently on sentence embeddings versus attention vectors.

That’s not a reason to avoid it. It’s a reason to benchmark on your own data first.

The RAG community is actively testing this. Theory is solid. 

Prod story for embeddings is still being written.

--- EXAMPLE 2 START ---

We removed the AI & system got smarter.

most teams using LLMs for classification are wasting money and calling it a day.

I've seen it firsthand. A pipeline calling an LLM API on every single message just to answer yes or no. Burning Tokens. Increasing latency. And the worst part? The system never got smarter. Every wrong prediction cost the same as the first one.

We've collectively agreed that LLMs = modern AI. So every new pipeline gets one by default. Nobody questions it because nobody wants to be the person who "didn't use AI."

But here's the thing nobody says out loud:

For classification problems with clear labels and continuous feedback, LLMs are often the worst tool you could pick.

Not because they can't do it. Because they don't learn from it.

We pulled the LLM out entirely and replaced it with:
• Sentence embeddings
• An SGD-based online classifier
• Confidence thresholds for edge cases
• Incremental updates from real user feedback

Millisecond latency. Near-zero inference cost. A model that actually improved over time instead of repeating the same mistakes at API prices.

Honestly, at this point, reaching for an LLM on anything AI-related isn't even a technical call. It's a social one. Feels safer. Easier to defend in a meeting. Nobody gets questioned for it.

Clear labels. repeated decisions. real feedback. that's not an LLM problem. that's a hammer problem. you're swinging a jackhammer.

Can AI Build Your App Without a Developer? Here’s the Real Answer.

A lot of non-technical founders are asking this right now and it’s a fair question.

AI tools like Gemini, Claude, and Cursor are genuinely impressive. But before you fire your developer, let’s understand what actually goes into building an app.

THE ANATOMY OF AN APP

Every app whether it’s a CRM, eCommerce store, or appointment booking system has two core layers:

Backend (the brain)
This is where your data lives and your business logic runs.

For a typical CRUD app (Create, Read, Update, Delete), the backend must handle:

• Authentication - Who are you?
• Authorisation - What are you allowed to do?
• Validation - Is the data you’re sending safe and correct?
• CRUD operations - The actual creation, reading, updating, and deleting of data

Beyond this sits servers, networking, routing, and scaling when your user base grows. Which we called DevOps



Frontend (the face)

This is what your users see and interact with. On the web, it’s HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. 

Modern apps use frameworks like React, Angular, or Vue.

You’ll also need to decide between Client-Side Rendering (fast, simpler) and Server-Side Rendering (more secure, harder to scale).


SO WHERE DOES AI FIT IN?

AI can write code. Good code, even. But here’s the catch to give AI the RIGHT instructions, you need to understand the trade-offs involved:

• Should I use client-side or server-side rendering?
• How do I isolate User A’s data from User B?
• How do I structure authorisation roles?

AI is a powerful executor. But it still needs a director.

THE HONEST ANSWER

For personal projects or simple prototypes? AI can get you surprisingly far.
For a commercial product where real users, real money, and real security are involved? 

You still need a developer not to write every line of code, but to make the architectural decisions AI can’t make on your behalf.

The developer’s role is shifting from builder to architect + AI director. 

That’s not going away anytime soon.
The future will be more accessible. 
But right now, direction still requires expertise.
